Three-body Collision in 1D

The goal of this simulation is to show different types of collisions in 1D, and how the interaction potential affects the dynamics. In 1D, two-body collisions are constrained to simple velocity exchange due to conservation laws, while three-body collisions can lead to nontrivial velocity redistribution. By adjusting the interaction potential and initial conditions, you can explore these different collision outcomes. For more details, refer to our PRA paper: Phys. Rev. A 110, 014114 (2024).

Two-body Collision in 1D (Velocity Exchange)

Two particles interact via a repulsive potential ~1/rⁿ. They approach, interact, and exchange velocities. Now try changing n to see how the interaction potential affects the collision dynamics.





Three-Body Collision in 1D (Energy Mixing)

Unlike two-body collisions, three-body collisions in 1D are not constrained to simple velocity exchange. Energy and momentum conservation are insufficient to fix the outcome, leading to nontrivial redistribution of velocities. In the following simulation, try to change the initial position of the rightmost particle to see how the final velocities change in a nontrivial way. Specifically, the middle particle is initialized at rest, try to have it moving at the end of the simulation by adjusting the initial position of the rightmost particle. One curious case is the n = 2 case, where there is a peculiar symmetry in the interaction potential that leads to a simple velocity exchange outcome for all initial conditions, even in the three-body case, and thus the middle particle always ends up at rest. Try changing n to see how this symmetry is broken and the middle particle can end up moving after the collision.





In contrast to the two-body case, three-body dynamics in 1D are not integrable, allowing sensitive dependence on initial conditions and nontrivial velocity redistribution.

Three-Body Recombination (Lennard-Jones Potential)

Here particles interact via a Lennard-Jones potential, combining short-range repulsion and long-range attraction. This allows formation of bound states, illustrating three-body recombination.

Energy can be redistributed such that two particles form a bound pair, while the third carries away excess energy—this is three-body recombination.
